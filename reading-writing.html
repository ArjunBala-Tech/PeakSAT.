HOW TO THINK ABOUT IT

Precision matters more than sounding smart.

The SAT rewards the answer that best matches the text, not the one that sounds the fanciest.

Read the exact wording

Small clue words can completely change the answer.

Use the passage

Answer from the text, not from outside opinion or memory.

Eliminate carefully

Often the best solve is proving why the other choices fail.

GRAMMAR RULES

Punctuation is a math formula.

The SAT tests strict grammatical rules. Memorize these three punctuation laws.

Semicolons (;) and Periods (.)

Grammatically identical on the SAT — both must separate two complete, independent sentences.

Trick: If two choices are identical except one has a period and one a semicolon — eliminate BOTH.

Colons (:)

Introduces a list, explanation, or quote. The sentence BEFORE the colon must be a complete, independent sentence.

Commas (,) with FANBOYS

To join two complete sentences with a comma, you MUST use FANBOYS (For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So).

Correct: "I studied, so I aced it."   Wrong: "I studied, I aced it." (Comma Splice)

STRATEGY

How to spot Trap Answers

The CollegeBoard writes incorrect answers using very specific patterns. Learn to spot them.

Extreme Language

Watch for always, never, impossible, prove. Correct answers are usually moderate: suggests, indicates, often.

Half-Right, All-Wrong

The first half matches perfectly, but the last three words ruin it. Read the entire choice.

True, but Irrelevant

It may be factually true in the real world — but if it's not in the passage, it's wrong.
